The world's fastest camera

In 2012 a team of scientists created something as nothing before: the trillion frames per second light-tracking camera.

The process, also named "femto-photography", consists of a continuous scanning of the object in order to create a single frame. To create a moving picture, a laser pulse was used (every 13 billionth of a second) to illuminate the scene; the laser and the camera were synchronised to make sure each pulse was identical.
So when the light that hit the object is reflected, the camera shoots at that exact moment capturing the image.
MIT scientists say the process can have practical uses, such analyzing fault, ultrafast chemical processes and so on.

The new world's fastest camera

Years pass, and MIT’s 1-trillion-frame-per-second camera is now outdated. Researchers in Sweden have created a new world’s fastest camera that shoots 5 trillion frames per second.

This amazing new camera called FRAME can make light "practically stand still" and capture events as short as 0.2 trillionths of a second. Scientists say the camera will be able to capture things in chemistry, physics, biology, and medicine that have never been caught on camera before, on a femtosecond scale.





A demonstration video

Researchers shot video of light photons traveling the distance of the thickness of a sheet of paper. This only takes a picosecond, but the camera can show the photons traveling by slowing things down.





Working

Each light pulse recorded gives a unique code. The object reflects light flashes into a single photograph, separated using an encryption key; several coded images give the result of one picture when they are put together.
